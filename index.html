Modular Ergonomic Back Cushion
Engineering Product Design Methodology · Northeastern University · 2024
People sit 6–10 hours daily, yet most ergonomic cushions are static — failing to adapt as users shift positions throughout the day. User research revealed that comfort isn't just about lumbar support, it's about adaptability across different sitting styles. This insight drove the design of a modular back cushion system with customizable support zones, developed over a full semester as part of the Engineering Product Design Methodology course at Northeastern University.
Design & Prototyping
Part & Assembly Sketches — AEarly ideation sketches exploring modular block geometry and adjustability mechanisms.
Part & Assembly Sketches — B1Refined sketches detailing component interfaces and modular attachment system.
Part & Assembly Sketches — B2Assembly sketches showing how modular blocks connect and adjust for different user profiles.
Final Design SketchesFinalized design sketches with dimensions and material callouts ahead of prototype build.
Final PrototypeCompleted modular ergonomic back cushion presented at end-of-semester review, demonstrating adjustability and user-centered design.
Project TeamTeam and professor at the final presentation of the Engineering Product Design Methodology course.
Design Process
  • User Research: Interviews and observation revealed that users shift positions constantly — making static cushions ineffective over time
  • Concept Development: Evaluated modular blocks vs. memory foam and adjustable strap configurations — selected modular system for adaptability and scalability
  • CAD & Design: Built SolidWorks models exploring multiple block geometries, optimizing for adjustability, stability, and ergonomic alignment
  • Prototyping: Progressed from low to high fidelity — testing block shapes, cushion placement, and strap mechanisms at each stage
  • Testing: Validated with 10 subjects over 2–3 hours — confirmed posture improvement and comfort gains, refined block shapes based on feedback
Key Outcomes
  • Conducted user interviews and observation — identified lack of personalization, discomfort over time, and poor fit across chairs as key pain points
  • Drove concept development — evaluated modular blocks vs. memory foam, adjustable straps, and multiple block geometries before finalizing direction
  • Built CAD models in SolidWorks, iterating on block geometry, adjustability, and ergonomic alignment across low, mid, and high fidelity prototypes
  • Tested with 10 subjects over 2–3 hours — observed posture improvement, positive response to customization, and feedback on block shape refinement
  • Collaborated across research and costing to balance desirability, feasibility, and viability throughout the design process

FDM Parameter Optimization
Mini Project · BNM Institute of Technology · 2022
A systematic study to optimize the best combination of FDM printing parameters for PETG material, using tensile strength as the performance metric. Two key parameters were varied — layer height (0.2mm & 0.3mm) and infill pattern (Grid, Triangle, Octet) — while infill density (60%) and shell thickness (1.2mm) were kept constant. Six parameter combinations were tested per ASTM D638 standards to identify optimal configurations for different application types.
Specimen Design & Preparation
CAD Model — ASTM D638 SpecimenDog-bone shaped test specimen modeled per ASTM D638 standard. Overall length: 165mm, gauge length: 50mm, width: 13mm, thickness: 3.2mm.
Slicing — Parameter ConfigurationThree infill patterns (Grid, Triangle, Octet) configured in Ultimaker Cura across two layer heights, producing 6 unique test configurations.
Printed Test SpecimensAll six PETG test specimens printed on Creality Ender 3 Pro, ready for tensile testing.
Testing & Results
Tensile Test SetupSpecimens tested on a universal testing machine per ASTM D638-14:2014. Load vs. cross head travel recorded for each configuration.
Specimens After TestingFractured specimens showing failure modes across different infill patterns and layer heights.
Test Results
  • Octet 0.3mm — Highest tensile stress: 34.58 N/mm² · Fracture point: 1.327 KN
  • Grid 0.3mm — Tensile stress: 22.78 N/mm² · Highest fracture point at 0.3mm layer height: 0.860 KN
  • Triangle 0.3mm — Tensile stress: 16.83 N/mm² · Fracture point: 0.610 KN
  • Octet 0.2mm — Tensile stress: 14.27 N/mm² · Fracture point: 0.539 KN
  • Grid 0.2mm — Tensile stress: 12.37 N/mm² · Fracture point: 0.474 KN
  • Triangle 0.2mm — Lowest tensile stress: 9.12 N/mm² · Fracture point: 0.349 KN
Key Outcomes
  • Octet infill at 0.3mm achieved 34.58 N/mm² — a 2.4× improvement over the weakest configuration
  • 0.3mm layer height consistently outperformed 0.2mm across all infill patterns
  • Recommended Octet/Grid at 0.3mm for aerospace tooling, jigs, fixtures, and functional parts
  • Recommended Triangle/low-density configs for dental guides, jewelry, and aesthetic prototypes
